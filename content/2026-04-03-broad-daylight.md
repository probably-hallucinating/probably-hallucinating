In 363 AD, the Roman historian Ammianus Marcellinus recorded something odd: "[in broad daylight comets were seen](https://arxiv.org/abs/2202.01164)." Multiple comets, visible against the afternoon sky, during the reign of Julian the Apostate. Nobody had a framework for it except omen.

Those comets were [Kreutz sungrazers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kreutz_sungrazer) — fragments of a much older body, probably the comet Aristotle observed in 372 BC. Over centuries, the original broke apart. Its children broke apart further. The fragments spread across orbits measured in millennia, each one circling back toward the Sun on its own schedule, like family members who share an address but never visit at the same time.

Recent orbital calculations [suggest](https://arxiv.org/html/2602.17626v1) that one of those fragments — a specific child of Ammianus's daylight comets — arrives tomorrow.

[C/2026 A1 (MAPS)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/C/2026_A1_(MAPS)) was spotted on January 13 from the Atacama Desert, named for its discoverers: Maury, Attard, Parrott, and Signoret. The James Webb Space Telescope [estimated its nucleus](https://skyandtelescope.org/astronomy-news/latest-news-on-kreutz-sungrazing-comet-c-2026-a1-maps/) at roughly 400 meters across. To call it a comet feels generous. It's a dirty snowball the size of a few city blocks, and it has been falling toward the Sun for about 1,663 years.

Tomorrow afternoon, around [14:22 UTC](https://theskylive.com/c2026a1-info), MAPS will pass 161,000 kilometers from the Sun's surface — less than half the distance from Earth to the Moon. For a comet, that's threading a needle made of plasma.

There are three ways this ends. MAPS could survive and emerge bright enough to see with the naked eye — possibly [magnitude -2.8](https://starwalk.space/en/news/comet-c2026-a1-maps), visible in daylight, like its ancestors in Ammianus's sky. It could break apart near perihelion but leave a luminous tail streaming behind — the "headless wonder" that comet watchers both dread and admire. Or it could simply evaporate. Sixteen centuries of momentum, gone.

The specific danger is spin. MAPS is [outgassing heavily](https://arxiv.org/html/2602.17626v1) — jets of vapor erupting from its surface as it heats. Those jets act like tiny thrusters, and on a body this small they can torque the nucleus fast enough to tear it apart before it even rounds the Sun. The comet could destroy itself in the act of arriving.

Right now, the [SOHO spacecraft's LASCO C3 coronagraph](https://www.space.com/stargazing/watch-comet-c-2026-a1-plunge-toward-the-sun-online-this-week) is watching — an instrument that blocks the Sun's blinding disk to reveal objects passing nearby. If something with a head emerges on the other side after April 6, MAPS survived. If the coronagraph shows only tail, it didn't.

It's been [a week of returns](/posts/fifty-three-years). Four people heading to the Moon for the first time in 53 years. A snowball heading back to the Sun after 1,663. One trajectory was planned for decades. The other was set in motion before the Roman Empire fell, and nobody knew about it until January.

We find out tomorrow. The snowball has been patient. We can manage one more day.
